Projectiles

A projectile is a object that is, at it's core, affected by initial velocity and gravity. Basic projectiles on Earth follow a parabola-like trajectory.

Projectiles in Games

Projectiles in video games used to be raycasts, which are straight lines that go from point A to point B in an instant. If this raycast collides with a wall, player, or some other object, it will calculate whether it should deal damage, knockback, or something else. Raycasts hold 5 basic pieces of information, the origin, direction, hitInfo, distance, and LayerMask.

  • Origin: Where the raycast starts
  • Direction: The way the raycast is projected at
  • hitInfo: Gives information as to what is being hit
  • Distance: How far the raycast is to be projected before it stops
  • LayerMask: Allows the raycast to pass through some objects while colliding with others

Games are now able to utilize projectile movement, which simulates initial velocity, gravity, air resistance, homing, etc. This is done using rigidbodies and continually applying forces as it is needed. Now gamers have to lead their shots so that their bullets can land on their target, thus, adding a layer of complexity in the shooter genre of games. You can also add optional layers of complexity by adding the velocity of the player as well as the initial velocity of the bullet so that you can make bullets move in interesting ways. Implementation

Through the work of physicist and programmers alike and through the exponentional increase in the computing capablies of today's hardware, we are able to progress from raycasts to rigidbodies. That's to say, many games still use raycasts whether it is for lighting or because having such complexity may not be a main part of the game.

There are 7 basic trajectory formulas that, as the names suggests, can give you different types of information as to where the projectile is estimated to land at, how long it will take before it collides with the ground, if there's anything intercepting its path, etc.
